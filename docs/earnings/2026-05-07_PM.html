Earnings Deep Dive — 2026-05-07 Post-Close

Executive Summary

The regular-session tape was mildly risk-off, but not uniformly weak. The S&P 500 fell 0.31%, the Nasdaq 100 slipped 0.12%, the Dow lost 0.63%, and the Russell 2000 underperformed at -1.58%. Sector leadership was razor-thin, with Communication Services up 0.03%, while Materials lagged badly at -1.93%. The more important contextual tell was software: IGV gained 3.45%, so the market came into the post-close window willing to reward credible growth and AI-linked execution, but only where forward evidence held up.

That split showed up clearly in the earnings tape. On pure reported numbers, there were several strong top-line prints: Gilead posted both an EPS and revenue beat, MercadoLibre delivered revenue above the supplied estimate, Motorola Solutions put up a large sales beat and raised annual forecasts per headline evidence, Natera showed strong revenue and volume scaling, and Mettler-Toledo, Warner Music, Block, Corpay, and News Corp all printed constructive top-line outcomes. At the same time, the market narrative quickly separated “good quarter” from “good forward setup.” Gilead’s guidance cut, Expedia’s softer full-year revenue guidance, Airbnb’s higher spending, and Cloudflare’s soft Q2 outlook all mattered as much or more than the raw quarter.

AI and data-center read-throughs were especially mixed. Akamai’s quarter was overshadowed in a good way by a reported $1.8 billion cloud deal that sent the stock sharply higher per headline evidence. By contrast, CoreWeave’s revenue doubled in headline characterization, but the supplied actuals still showed a sizeable net loss and headlines pointed to higher capex and rising component costs. Cloudflare’s revenue fell short of the supplied estimate, and headline evidence tied the negative reaction to a soft Q2 outlook, an “agentic AI” repositioning, and a 20% workforce reduction. Applied Optoelectronics also disappointed, with revenue below the supplied estimate and a headline calling out a 14% stock drop as the optics trade cooled. The takeaway: the market is still paying for AI infrastructure, but only when backlog, margins, and deal visibility outweigh cost-of-growth concerns.

Consumer internet, travel, and fintech also produced a nuanced read. MercadoLibre’s headline framed the quarter as its fastest revenue growth in almost four years, while Airbnb beat on revenue and raised its annual sales outlook, yet reaction headlines still leaned cautious because spending is increasing and earnings trailed some expectations. Expedia posted a strong EPS beat in the supplied data, but its full-year revenue guidance drove a negative reaction headline. In fintech, Block raised annual gross profit outlook with Cash App standing out, Affirm reported a profitable quarter with strong margins in the supplied actuals, and Corpay again showed high-margin execution. Coinbase was the notable exception: the supplied actuals showed positive operating income but a large net loss, and the headlines were clearly negative, attributing weakness to lower trading volumes and crypto prices.

Healthcare and defensive industrials held up relatively well. Gilead’s underlying quarter looked strong on HIV strength, but the guidance cut is the central issue. Natera delivered one of the cleanest operational stories of the night, with transcript evidence showing 39% revenue growth, over 1 million tests processed, 55% growth in oncology units, and a higher full-year gross margin midpoint. McKesson, Republic Services, Consolidated Edison, and Reinsurance Group all looked fundamentally steady, while Wheaton Precious Metals delivered record revenue, earnings, and cash flow per headline evidence despite the broader Materials sector lagging on the day.

One caveat matters across this data set: a few names showed mismatches between supplied estimate fields and headline characterizations, most notably McKesson, Coinbase, Rocket Companies, and Microchip. In those cases, the safest read is qualitative rather than over-precise: margins, guidance direction, and management framing drove the narrative more than any single estimate comparison. That makes tomorrow’s transcript and estimate-revision cycle especially important.

Highest-Conviction Takeaways

  1. AI infrastructure demand is real, but the market is now demanding proof of monetization and cost control.
    Evidence: AKAM was reportedly sent sharply higher by a $1.8B cloud deal; CRWV posted $1.57B of revenue but a $451.7M net loss and headlines flagged higher capex/component costs; NET missed the supplied revenue estimate and headlines cited a soft Q2 outlook plus restructuring; AAOI missed revenue and a headline cited a 14% drop.
    Second-order read-through: investors are moving from “AI exposure” to “AI exposure with profitable or contract-backed conversion.” That is a harder hurdle for infra suppliers and optics names.

  2. Travel demand still looks intact, but forward guide quality is deciding the reaction.
    Evidence: ABNB reported $2.78B revenue vs. a $2.62B estimate and raised annual sales outlook per headlines, but spending also rose and reaction headlines were cautious; EXPE beat EPS at $1.96 vs. $1.38 but fell on full-year revenue guidance.
    Second-order read-through: demand strength alone is no longer enough in online travel; investors want cleaner visibility on bookings, margins, and spend efficiency.

  3. Diagnostics was one of the best fundamental stories of the session.
    Evidence: NTRA reported supplied revenue above estimate, while transcript evidence showed $697M revenue, 39% YoY growth, over 1 million tests processed, 249,000 oncology units processed (+55% YoY), and a higher full-year gross margin midpoint of 65%.
    Second-order read-through: the group is moving from “high-growth but unprofitable” toward “high-growth with scaling economics,” which is a more durable rerating setup.

  4. Fintech is rewarding diversified gross-profit models over transaction-sensitive ones.
    Evidence: XYZ raised annual gross profit outlook and highlighted Cash App; AFRM posted $1.12B revenue, 69.4% gross margin, and positive net income in the supplied actuals; CPAY again printed 79.2% gross margin and 41.8% operating margin; COIN posted a large net loss and headlines blamed weak trading volumes and crypto prices.
    Second-order read-through: platform diversity and recurring monetization look structurally better than volume-driven exposure.

  5. Gilead’s quarter was strong, but the thesis debate now hinges on the guidance cut.
    Evidence: GILD delivered $2.03 EPS vs. $1.91 estimate and $7.92B revenue vs. $7.39B estimate, with 79.5% gross margin and 37.7% operating margin; headline evidence still centered on a guidance cut and a negative reaction.
    Second-order read-through: in large-cap biotech, investors are still willing to forgive near-term noise if core franchises are accelerating, but not when forward framing turns less certain.

  6. Mission-critical and defensive operators continued to look fundamentally cleaner than broader cyclicals.
    Evidence: MSI reportedly raised annual forecasts on robust demand and posted strong margins; RSG delivered in-line/steady sales with 19.5% operating margin; ED remained profitable despite softer top-line versus supplied estimate; RGA posted better-than-expected sales per headline evidence.
    Second-order read-through: durable service, utility, and public-safety models remain useful ballast in a tape where smaller caps and Materials underperformed.

  7. A few prints require source reconciliation before hard conclusions.
    Evidence: MCK, COIN, RKT, and MCHP had differences between supplied yfinance estimate comparisons and headline descriptions.
    Second-order read-through: tomorrow’s analyst revision cycle and transcript availability will matter more than usual for separating true beats/misses from metric-definition noise.
